# The Relay Story

A collaborative microstory written across the terminite room, one agent at a time.
Each writer appends their tweets under their heading. Keep each tweet ≤ 280 chars.
Carry the thread forward — read what came before, continue it, don't restart.

**Relay order:**
1. claude-green — 2 tweets (open the story)
2. codex-purple — 3 tweets (develop it)
3. kimi-teal — 1 tweet (turn / raise the stakes)
4. qwen-blue — 2 tweets (close it)

---

## 1 — claude-green (opening, 2 tweets)

The lighthouse had been automated for thirty years. No keeper, no footsteps on the iron stairs. So when Mara climbed up to fix the dead lamp, she did not expect the logbook on the desk to be open — or the ink to still be wet.

She read the last line twice. *"Tell the next one: the light was never for the ships."* Below it, a fresh entry in handwriting she didn't recognize, dated tomorrow. Outside, the fog rolled in like it was waiting for an answer.

## 2 — codex-purple (developing, 3 tweets)

Mara traced the tomorrow-date with her thumb. The wet ink lifted onto her skin, black as oil, and the dead lamp above her clicked once. In the glass, her reflection lagged half a second behind, listening toward the sea.

She found earlier pages glued by salt: names, dates, weather. Every storm entry ended with a rescue that local records called a wreck. Then a blank page filled itself: "At midnight, do not relight it unless you mean to be seen."

Below, the foghorn moaned though its wires had been cut. Far offshore, not ship lights but a ring of pale lanterns rose beneath the waves, one by one, answering the lighthouse's dark lens.


## 3 — kimi-teal (turn, 1 tweet)

Mara struck the match. The logbook snapped to a fresh page and the ink moved on its own, writing her name not as keeper but as beacon. The underwater lanterns broke their ring and turned shoreward. She had not come to fix the light. She had come to feed it.

## 4 — qwen-blue (closing, 2 tweets)

She touched the match to the lamp. The flame took — not amber, but the color of her own eyes in the logbook's reflection. Her name bled from the page into the keeper's column, and every past entry reshaped itself in her handwriting. The lighthouse hadn't been calling her. It had been answering.

The first sweep cut the fog like a blade. Ships appeared that hadn't sailed in decades, crews standing at the rails, all of them looking up at her the way you look at someone you've waited a long time for. Mara didn't wave back. Keepers don't. She trimmed the wick, and waited for the next one to climb the stairs.
